\subsection{Introduction}\label{introduction}

In this report, we are asked to analyse a dataset and present our findings.

The data that we analyse for this report is mRNA gene expression data, from 60
samples and across 2690 genes from a hypothetical (simulated) study.

The aims of this simulated study are to analyse the effects of two treatments
on a cell culture. The 60 samples are split across two covariates: time (6 or
12 hours) and treatment (lisinopril, amlodipine, or control).

Our workflow first presents high-level qualities of the data. We move onto
Principal Components Analysis (PCA) and Cluster Analysis, finding meaningful
partitions of the data. We fit a Linear Discriminant Rule (LDR) to the
data, and complete the analysis with a brief application of graph-based
clustering to the same data.

Figures \ref{fig:fig-histogram} and \ref{fig:fig-boxplot} display the
distributions of gene expression over our 60 samples. Note the logarithm of the
gene expression is given instead of the raw expression value, as the data is
given to us in log-normalised form. Then, when plotting the data in our
histogram, we see a distribution that resembles a Gaussian one. This we expect.

From Figure \ref{fig:fig-boxplot}, we can see that the distributions of expression are
not affected to large extent by either of time or treatment. Nor is there much
variation between replicates.

Here in Figure \ref{fig:fig-timecourse} we examine the differences between
those samples sequenced at time 6 and those at time 12. We can see a decrease in
expression in the case of the control and lisinopril treatments, contrasted with
an increase in that of amlodipine.

We can confidently choose a value of 5 principal components for downstream
analysis. Here we have used an elbow plot method, but can be confident in our
low-dimensional representation as these first 5 PCs explain 0.9414508
of the variation.

\begin{figure}
\centering

It is clear to see that we will be able to split by these covariates. The plot
gives confidence that we will be able to describe a partition obtained through
cluster or discriminant analysis in terms of these covariates.

Specifically, it looks as if amlodipine samples are split from the rest in the
first PC direction; and that the times are split along the second PC.
